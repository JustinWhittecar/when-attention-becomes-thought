# Why I Wrote This

*Last updated: 2026-04-24.*

I am from a generation that grew up discovering the internet. I still remember the first time I used MapQuest to print out directions, and the wonder I felt when those printouts eventually became step-by-step instructions dictated by my phone. Most of my generation learned to write at least some HTML. Not because we were taught it in school; most of our teachers were too scared of Wikipedia ruining research skills to learn even the basics. We learned HTML because it was social currency. We shared our thoughts on LiveJournal and worried about which top-eight friends we'd appear in. The internet didn't feel like a revolution while we were living it. It felt like a novelty, much the way today's chatbots can still feel like novelties when you forget what they are already doing.

Even so, I was always the sci-fi kid. I grew up devouring books like _Snow Crash_ and _Neuromancer_ and fell asleep dreaming of flying cars and humanoid robots. To my people, Alan Turing was a celebrity on the scale of any president or pop star. My basement still has a server I use for AI experiments, named after an Isaac Asimov character. And yet I got a degree in English. I was convinced I was going to be a writer, eventually. My obsession with AI took over first.

Out of college I started as a marketer, writing python scripts to automate the tedious parts of my job. That habit led, over time, to product management. For most of my career I've worked at AI companies or on teams building AI-powered tools, almost always focused on sales and marketing. I grew up in a family of engineers, and I've usually been the only humanities major on the team. My role, over and over, was translation. I took what the data scientists and engineers were building and rendered it into language that sales reps could actually use with customers. In the mid-2010s that meant predictive modeling and recommendation engines; after ChatGPT, it meant LLMs. Then I carried the customer's real problems back to the people building the product.

One moment from that work has stayed with me. In the early days of ChatGPT, the stickiest problem my team was facing was how to reliably figure out which words actually carried meaning in a long piece of text. We had built a dozen workarounds over many months, and ChatGPT blew every one of them out of the water in an afternoon. I'm not a researcher or an engineer. I've always been the person in the room making sure the technical and the human sides could talk to each other. But after that afternoon, I could not stop paying attention.

That career gave me a front-row seat as these models grew from a narrow tool for translation into something that touches lives in ways more profound than anything from my childhood. But for a long time, I was convinced the biggest claims about them were noise.

I have owned a Tesla for years. I believed in what the company was building enough to put money behind it, and I wanted the technology to work. So I watched, and waited, and listened to the promises. Full Self-Driving was imminent. Then it was "feature complete." Then it was arriving in a software update, just a few weeks out. I sat in the driver's seat with my hands hovering over the wheel on a clear stretch of highway and thought, okay, this is genuinely impressive. And then I hit a construction zone, or an unmapped road, or rain after dark, and the car handed control back to me with a chime, as if nothing had happened. The gap between the promise and the experience taught me something I filed away and kept: the people with the most to sell are usually the ones making the biggest claims. I became, as a matter of professional habit, skeptical of anyone announcing that the future had finally arrived.

That skepticism held, more or less, through most of 2024.

Then, in the fall, OpenAI released o1.

I remember giving it a problem I expected it to fail at, a messy, multi-step reasoning task that previous models would have papered over with confident-sounding nonsense. It didn't fail. It worked through the problem the way a careful person might: flagging its own uncertainty, backing up when a step didn't hold, trying a different angle. It wasn't perfect. But the failures, when they came, were different. More honest, more bounded. Something had shifted in a way I couldn't quite dismiss. I pushed harder over the following weeks. The shift held.

A few months after that, I started reading more carefully about what was happening _inside_ these models. The mechanistic interpretability research unsettled me in the best way. Researchers weren't finding a black box that defied explanation. They were finding structure. Circuits that reliably detect specific features. Attention heads doing identifiable, nameable jobs. Behavior that looked less like statistical averaging and more like reasoning assembled from recognizable parts. I wasn't sure what to make of it. But it was harder to look at and shrug.

Then I stumbled on the article "Does AI Already Have Human-Level Intelligence? The Evidence Is Clear" in _Nature_. I read it expecting to push back. Instead, I found myself nodding more often than I wanted to. The authors, a team spanning philosophy, machine learning, linguistics, and cognitive science, argued that by any reasonable standard, including Turing's own, AI systems now qualify as generally intelligent. They pulled evidence from across several fields. One number in particular stayed with me: in March 2025, GPT-4.5 was judged to be human 73% of the time in a formal Turing test, more often than actual humans were judged to be human (Jones & Bergen, 2025). By February 2026, the authors concluded directly that "the long-standing problem of creating AGI has been solved" (Chen et al., 2026).

I am not certain they are right. A synthesis is not a proof. The Turing test has been gamed before, and what looks like reasoning today may yet turn out to be something subtler under tomorrow's microscope. The interpretability findings I am leaning on are real, but the field is young; circuits we name confidently this year tend to look messier the next. I have spent enough years around this technology to know that the most confident reading of any single paper is usually the one that ages the worst.

What I am certain of is that the question has become serious enough to take seriously. A few years ago I would have rolled my eyes at "AGI has been solved" appearing under a major journal's masthead. After the o1 cascade, the interpretability work, and the Chen synthesis, I cannot anymore. Something has changed. The right response is not to celebrate or to dismiss but to look closely. That is what this book is, and that is the strange position I find myself writing from: not converted, not dismissive, persuaded that the question matters more than I want it to.

If I'm going to tell that story properly, I have to go back to 2017, and to a single architectural innovation in how we approached processing text for translation: the transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017). The mechanism of self-attention, which allows every element in a sequence to attend to every other element in parallel, replaced the sequential bottleneck of recurrent networks and unlocked a new relationship between computation, data, and capability. What followed was not a single breakthrough but a chain of them, each building on the last in ways that were sometimes predicted and sometimes genuinely surprising. Models trained on this architecture went on to win gold at the International Mathematical Olympiad (OpenAI, 2025; Google DeepMind, 2025), collaborate with leading mathematicians on new theorems and generate scientific hypotheses validated in experiments (Bubeck et al., 2025), solve PhD-level examination problems (Rein et al., 2023; Phan et al., 2026), and compose poetry that readers preferred to human-written work (Porter & Machery, 2024). None of that was obvious in 2017.

From the transformer, the chain unfolded in stages. Scaling laws turned bigger models into qualitatively new ones; alignment work taught them to follow instructions; reasoning techniques gave them something like multi-step thought. Interpretability research began finding structure inside what had looked like a black box. Agentic systems pushed these models out of the chat window and into the world. By the time we come back to the AGI question itself, the evidence will be on the table for you to weigh.

I want to be clear about one thing before we begin. I'm committed to presenting the strongest versions of both sides. Where the evidence supports the AGI claim, I'll make that case fully and without hedging. Where serious researchers push back (and they do push back, with serious arguments), I'll give those objections their full weight, not as a disclaimer buried at the end but as part of the argument itself. A claim this significant deserves that much. My goal isn't to bring you around to a particular conclusion; it's to leave you informed. I'm committed not to your adoption of AI, but to your learning about it.

This book is for anyone who wants to understand how this technology came to be. Not just enthusiasts or engineers, but anyone who has looked at what these systems can now do and wondered, seriously, how we got here. It is an attempt to make sense of both worlds well enough to explain either one. My hope is that by the end, some of the magic behind the curtain will be visible: the chain of ideas, built across nearly a decade, that may have done something no one in 2017 would have dared predict.
